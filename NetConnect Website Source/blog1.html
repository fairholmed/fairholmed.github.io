The Budget 2016: Mobile Technology

Posted by Mat Gilman

There was more to George Osborne’s 2016 budget than just the introduction of a new “Sugar Tax”. On Wednesday the Chancellor included a number of Mobile Telecoms items in his speech.

5G

The Chancellor noted that the UK has the highest internet usage of any G7 country. The government is committed to deliver a 5G strategy in 2017, based on an assessment by the National Infrastructure Commission of how the UK can become a world leader in 5G. The National Infrastructure Commission has been asked to consider by the end of 2016 what the UK needs to do to become a world leader in 5G infrastructure deployment, and to ensure that the UK can take early advantage of the potential benefits of 5G services.

Osborne also announced that the government committing that 750MHz of valuable public sector spectrum in bands under 10GHz will be made available by 2022, of which 500MHz will be made available by 2020. This builds on government’s previous 2010 commitment, by supporting innovation in digital communications services and the development of new technologies. This reflects Ofcoms expectations that 5G will make use of frequency bands above 6GHz.

The government will also support the development of a network planning tool, to be trialled in Bournemouth.

4G

Mobile Operators in the UK are still required to ensure 98% of the population can access 4G, by the end of 2017. Whilst some operators are closer to achieving this than others, the remaining challenges are mostly coverage for the rural population.

In his budget, George Osborne vowed to change planning regulations in an attempt to improve connectivity. Masts up to 25 metres (20 metres in protected areas) will no longer require planning permission (currently the limit is 10 metres). This will most greatly benefit rural areas.
